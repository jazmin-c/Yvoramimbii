The Journey to Earth

One day, I got really excited and jumped out with more force. I traveled as a gigantic wave of particles and magnetic field: this is what humans call a coronal mass ejection (CME). My trip across vast space is an adventure full of speed and energy.

So... What does CME mean?

Lumi explained it to you: How does the gigantic wave of particles that comes from the Sun travel?

💡 Fact Unlocked: A CME (like Lumi) travels at a speed of up to 3,000 kilometers per second, crossing the distance from the Sun to Earth in just a couple of days!

Auroras and Dancing Lights

And then, the magic happens! Upon reaching Earth, I encounter its invisible shield: the magnetic field.. When my energy and particles collide with the atmosphere, they paint the sky with dancing, colorful lights. It's an amazing spectacle known as auroras, a grand light show at the North Pole and the South Pole!

Extra Fact:Auroras occur at about 100 to 300 kilometers in altitude—right where the atmosphere meets space!

🔭 Eyes on the Sun: The Guardians of NASA

But don't be scared! Earth has very intelligent guardians. Scientists and agencies like NASA have the mission to observe my home. Thanks to advanced satellites, they can see me leave the Sun, even before I get close to Earth.

This constant monitoring allows them to warn everyone in time, making my "mischief" just a beautiful light show.

Learn About the NASA Tools That Help Scientists with Space Weather

SDO is NASA's eye that watches the Sun 24/7. It sends images and data that allow scientists to see Lumi (CME) at the exact moment it leaves the Sun. It's crucial for early warnings.

This is the most daring spacecraft. On a mission to "touch the Sun," NASA's Parker Solar Probe became the first spacecraft to fly through the corona (the Sun's upper atmosphere) in 2021. Its mission is to touch and analyze the solar wind and CMEs, giving us essential information from Lumi's battlefield.

SOHO has been our sentinel since 1995. Located at a strategic point, it monitors space weather. Without SOHO, we wouldn't know much of what happens on the Sun.

How Do We Protect Ourselves?

Scientists observe the Sun with advanced telescopes and special satellites to notify us when my friends and I are visiting. This way, aviators, power companies, and space agencies can prepare, protecting their equipment and, most importantly, the people.
